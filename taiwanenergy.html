Gridlocked: Energy Security, Developmental Politics, and the Local Crisis of Renewable Energy in Taiwan

The paper is forthcoming in the Rice Asian Studies Review, Volume 9. A pre-publication draft is available via the link below.
Read draft (Google Drive) Rice Asian Studies Review

Abstract

Taiwan's renewable energy transition presents a compelling case study on the relationship between energy security, governance, and local implementation. As a small island importing 98% of its energy supply, Taiwan is highly exposed to global market volatility and particularly vulnerable to the growing geopolitical tensions with China. With strong coastal winds, a sunny climate, and a strategic position on the Pacific Ring of Fire, the island has the potential to develop a robust renewable energy industry that could meaningfully strengthen its energy security. Yet despite the urgent security imperatives, Taiwan achieved only 11.6% of its electricity from renewable sources by 2024, falling dramatically short of its 20% target and well below the global average of 30%.

This paper argues that Taiwan's underperformance reflects, in part, deep structural contradictions rooted in its governance model. Examining the geopolitical imperatives driving the transition, the post-developmental institutional structures shaping its strategy, and the local-level tensions undermining its implementation, this paper finds that the challenges Taiwan faces stem from the difficulty of reconciling centralized, top-down energy governance with a transition that requires meaningful adaptation to local contexts. Taiwan's case thus speaks to a broader challenge faced by states pursuing rapid renewable transitions. Energy security imperatives alone do not guarantee effective implementation when governance frameworks remain misaligned with the decentralized, place-based demands of renewable energy systems.
