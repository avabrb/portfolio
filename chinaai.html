Human-AI Relations in China: Governance, Philosophy, and Disability

Flower lily on the Thamalakane River, in Maun, Botswana.
Photo taken on December 17th, 2025

Abstract

Since releasing the “New Generation Artificial Intelligence Development Plan” in July 2017, China has projected to become a global leader in artificial intelligence by 2030—rapidly expanding its influence in research, development, and governance. China’s primary goal through AI policies is to foster innovation and “common prosperity,” as opposed to Western powers like the U.S., which promotes technological innovation for improved productivity and business competitiveness, and the E.U., which emphasizes AI ethics to safeguard individual rights.

Despite abundant research on China’s state-led AI policies, less attention is paid to how cultural norms, philosophical traditions, and governance systems shape individual attitudes toward AI and human–AI relations. To address this gap, I propose examining how Chinese citizens imagine the risks, benefits, and roles of AI, and how cultural philosophies and governance systems may shape these perceptions, contributing to more pluralistic and globally inclusive debates on AI ethics and human-AI relations.
